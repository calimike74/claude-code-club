# Working With AI Outputs — A Mini-Lesson Series

> **The problem:** Students prompt Claude, get impressive-looking spreadsheets or reports, and then don't know what to do with them. These short lessons teach the skill that sits between "getting output" and "using it."

---

## How to Use These Lessons

Each lesson is 20-30 minutes. They can run in order or standalone. Every lesson follows the same pattern:

1. **Get an output** (5 min) — prompt Claude for something specific
2. **Interrogate it** (10 min) — ask questions about what you got
3. **Do something with it** (10 min) — make a decision, change something, present a finding

The third step is the key. AI output without action is just noise.

---

## Lesson 1: "What Am I Looking At?"

**Skill:** Reading and interpreting AI-generated data

**Setup:** Each student prompts Claude:
> "Create a monthly budget spreadsheet for a 22-year-old graduate starting their first job in London earning $28,000. Include rent, transport, food, savings, and discretionary spending."

**Interrogation questions** (students ask Claude these as follow-ups):
- "Which of these numbers did you calculate and which did you estimate?"
- "What assumptions did you make about rent in London? Are they realistic?"
- "Show me which line items I could cut to save $200/month — rank them by impact"

**The action:** Each student identifies the ONE assumption in the budget they think is most wrong, corrects it, and explains why to a partner.

**What they're learning:** AI doesn't label its guesses vs. its calculations. You have to ask.

---

## Lesson 2: "What If?"

**Skill:** Using AI outputs as a starting point for scenario analysis

**Setup:** Students use the budget from Lesson 1 (or generate a fresh one). Then:
> "What happens to this budget if interest rates rise from 4.5% to 7%? Show me the knock-on effects."

**Interrogation questions:**
- "Which expenses are affected by interest rates and which aren't?"
- "If I wanted to keep my savings rate the same, what would I have to cut?"
- "Compare this to what would happen if inflation hit 8% instead"

**The action:** Students create a simple "decision memo" (3-5 bullet points) answering: "If you were this person, what would you change about your financial plan and why?" They write this themselves — not Claude.

**What they're learning:** The spreadsheet isn't the answer. It's the starting point for your own thinking.

---

## Lesson 3: "Prove It"

**Skill:** Fact-checking and validating AI outputs against real data

**Setup:** Students prompt Claude:
> "Give me a comparison table of the top 5 UK savings accounts available right now, with interest rates, minimum deposits, and access restrictions."

**Interrogation questions:**
- "When was this information last verified?"
- "Are these rates still current as of today?"
- Students then spend 5 minutes checking 2-3 of the rates against actual bank websites

**The action:** Mark up the table — green for correct, amber for close, red for wrong. Discuss: How much was accurate? What was outdated?

**What they're learning:** AI can hallucinate specific numbers. Financial data especially needs verification. The skill is knowing *when* to check, not checking everything.

---

## Lesson 4: "Make It Mine"

**Skill:** Customising generic AI outputs into something personally useful

**Setup:** Students prompt Claude:
> "Create a personal finance dashboard template I can use monthly. Include income tracking, expense categories, savings goals, and a net worth tracker."

**Interrogation questions:**
- "I don't have investments or a mortgage yet — remove those sections and replace them with student-relevant categories"
- "Add a section for tracking my part-time job hours and pay"
- "Change the savings goal to saving for [something specific to them — driving lessons, a laptop, uni deposit]"

**The action:** Each student ends with a customised template that reflects *their* actual situation, not a generic one. They should be able to explain every section.

**What they're learning:** Default AI output is generic. The value comes from iterating until it matches your reality.

---

## Lesson 5: "Explain It To Someone Else"

**Skill:** Translating AI analysis into plain-language communication

**Setup:** Students prompt Claude:
> "Analyse whether it's better to rent or buy a flat in Birmingham for a couple earning $55,000 combined. Include a 5-year cost comparison."

Claude will produce a detailed analysis with numbers, assumptions, and a recommendation.

**The action (this is the whole lesson):**
Students rewrite Claude's analysis as ONE of these:
- A 60-second explanation to a friend who asked "should I buy or rent?"
- A 3-slide presentation to a family member considering the same decision
- A short social media post summarising the key finding

They cannot copy-paste from Claude. They must translate it into their own words for their chosen audience.

**What they're learning:** Understanding something well enough to explain it simply is the real test. If you can't explain the AI's output, you don't understand it yet.

---

## Lesson 6: "Challenge the AI"

**Skill:** Constructive disagreement with AI recommendations

**Setup:** Students prompt Claude:
> "I'm 17, I have $500 saved, and I want to start investing. What should I do?"

Claude will give advice. Then:

**Interrogation questions:**
- "What are the risks you didn't mention?"
- "Is a 17-year-old actually allowed to open an investment account in the UK?"
- "What would a financial advisor say about this advice if I showed it to them?"
- "Give me the counter-argument — why might this be bad advice?"

**The action:** Students write a "two-sided brief" — Claude's recommendation on one side, the strongest argument against it on the other. They decide which side they agree with and write one sentence explaining why.

**What they're learning:** AI gives confident-sounding advice. Confidence isn't correctness. The skill is in pushing back.

## Running These Lessons

### Timing
- Each lesson: 20-30 minutes
- Can run one per session alongside other club activities
- Or batch 2-3 together for a focused "AI Literacy" session

### What Students Need
- Access to Claude (claude.ai, Claude Desktop, or Claude Code)
- Nothing else — no special setup, no coding

### Assessment (Light Touch)
The "action" step in each lesson produces something concrete. If you want to track progress:
- Lesson 1: Their corrected assumption (shows critical reading)
- Lesson 2: Their decision memo (shows independent analysis)
- Lesson 3: Their marked-up table (shows verification instinct)
- Lesson 4: Their customised template (shows iteration skill)
- Lesson 5: Their plain-language translation (shows comprehension)
- Lesson 6: Their two-sided brief (shows balanced evaluation)

### Linking to A-Level Specifications

These lessons connect to:
- **Business Studies:** Financial planning, cash flow, investment appraisal, decision-making frameworks
- **Economics:** Interest rates, inflation, housing markets, opportunity cost, behavioural economics (AI confidence bias)
- **Critical Thinking / EPQ:** Source evaluation, argument construction, evidence-based reasoning
