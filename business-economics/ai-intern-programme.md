# Claude Code Club — Business & Economics Track

## Context

Two Year 12 boys have joined the Claude Code Club but split from the main group (who build websites and apps). They want to learn how AI applies to business and economics careers but aren't sure exactly what that looks like. They've had one week of sessions on PowerShell and Claude Code, so they're comfortable in the terminal.

Mike (music technology teacher, strong AI/Claude Code skills) runs the club. He has no business teaching background, so the programme needs to be self-sufficient — Mike facilitates rather than teaches business content. The materials must be engaging enough that students stay interested without needing to self-direct.

## Goals

1. **AI literacy for business careers** — students understand what AI tools can do in professional business contexts
2. **Tangible deliverables** — each mission produces something real (reports, presentations, analyses)
3. **Portable skills** — students leave with reusable AI workflows, not just completed tasks
4. **Engagement from session one** — the first mission must create a "wow" moment that brings them back
5. **Flexible pacing** — sessions breathe between structured missions and open discovery

## Design

### Two-Track Structure

The programme runs two parallel tracks within each session. The balance shifts naturally depending on the week.

#### Track A: "The AI Intern" — Business Missions

A sequence of realistic business scenarios. Each casts the student in a professional role, gives them a brief from a fictional manager, and asks them to produce a deliverable using Claude Code. Missions are self-contained — skipping or reordering doesn't break anything.

**Mission sequence:**

| # | Mission | Role | Deliverable | Key AI Skills |
|---|---------|------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | Market Entry Briefing | Junior Analyst | One-page market briefing with visuals | Research, professional write-up, visual generation, iteration |
| 2 | Competitor Teardown | Strategy Intern | SWOT analysis and comparison presentation | Deep company analysis, structured frameworks, presentation building |
| 3 | The Pitch | Startup Founder | Pitch deck for a business idea | Idea development, deck creation, persuasive writing |
| 4 | Automate the Office | Operations Manager | Automated workflow demos | Document summarisation, data processing, report generation |
| 5 | Board Report | Finance Analyst | Executive summary with charts from real data | Data retrieval, chart generation, executive writing register |
| 6 | The Portfolio | Themselves | Personal AI portfolio | Curation, self-presentation, reflection |

**Arc:** Consuming/analysing (missions 1-2) → Creating/pitching (mission 3) → Automating/operating (missions 4-5) → Owning it (mission 6).

**Each mission includes:**
- An interactive brief (visually engaging, designed to feel professional and exciting — not a plain text handout)
- A clear, bounded deliverable
- 2-3 AI skills that get added to the student's personal Playbook
- Optional extension work they can continue between sessions

#### Track B: Discovery & Exploration

The organic layer that runs alongside missions. This is where:
- Mike shows quick demos from his notes ("look what Claude Code can do with a spreadsheet")
- Students ask questions about how Claude Code works
- Mike pulls up a 5-minute topic: "here's how a consulting firm actually uses AI"
- Students explore something that caught their interest
- Tips on creating AI workflows for business, research, A-level work

Track B draws from Mike's existing club notes (Cowork prompts, API lists, SaaS structures, economics research links) and grows as students surface their own questions.

**Track B keeps sessions alive** when students aren't in mission mode and prevents the programme from feeling like a rigid syllabus.

### Session Flow

No fixed formula. The balance between tracks shifts naturally:

| Session energy | Approach |
|---------------|----------|
| Engaged and ready | Brief a mission, let them run, debrief at the end |
| Curious and questioning | Discovery mode — demos, Q&A, exploration |
| Flat or unfocused | Short punchy demo of something impressive, then ease into a mission |
| Between sessions | Students can continue missions from home if they want |

### The AI Playbook

Each student maintains a personal markdown file that grows every session. After each session they add:
- What they learned (in their own words)
- The prompts and workflows that worked
- Ideas for what they'd use this for in the future

The Playbook serves three purposes:
1. **Practical** — a reference they actually use when working with AI
2. **Portfolio** — evidence of AI literacy for uni applications and personal statements
3. **Continuity** — Mike can see what they've covered even if he misses context

### A-Level Connections (Light Touch)

Not forced into every session, but pointed out when natural:
- Market sizing → Economics (market structures, demand, price elasticity)
- Competitor analysis → Business (Porter's Five Forces, competitive advantage)
- Data analysis → Economics (interpreting real macroeconomic indicators)
- Pitch decks → Business (marketing mix, value propositions, USPs)
- Financial reports → Economics (GDP, inflation, interest rate data)

### Mission 1 Detail: Market Entry Briefing

**The scenario:**
> You've just started as a junior analyst at a consulting firm. A client is considering launching a new product into the UK market — a subscription-based AI tutoring app for A-level students. Your manager needs a one-page market entry briefing on her desk by end of day.
>
> She wants: market size estimate, 3 key competitors with brief profiles, one clear recommendation (go or no-go), and a visual summary she can show the client.

**Why this works for session one:**
- The product is something they understand as A-level students themselves
- It touches research, analysis, writing, AND visuals — showing Claude Code's range in one hit
- The deliverable is tangible — they walk out with a professional-looking briefing
- Going from nothing to a polished market briefing in 40 minutes is the "wow" moment

**AI skills learned:**
- Briefing Claude Code on a research task with clear parameters
- Getting Claude to structure findings into a professional format
- Generating visual summaries (charts, comparison tables, infographics)
- Iterating on output ("make this more concise," "add a SWOT," "reformat as a one-pager")

**Playbook entry after mission 1:**
- How to prompt for business research
- How to ask for professional formatting
- How to generate and refine visuals
- The iteration loop: rough output → refine → polish

## Deliverables To Build

1. **Interactive mission brief template** — a single-page HTML file (viewable in browser) for presenting scenarios. Reusable across all missions. Must feel professional and exciting, not like a school handout. Styled with modern design, animations or transitions where appropriate.
2. **Mission 1 brief** — the Market Entry Briefing, fully written and designed using the template
3. **AI Playbook template** — starter markdown file for students with sections for each session
4. **Discovery bank** — 8-12 quick demo topics, each with a one-paragraph setup and a suggested Claude Code prompt to try

## Constraints

- Mike is not a business teacher — materials must be self-explanatory and not require business domain expertise to facilitate
- Students are Year 12 (16-17), comfortable with Claude Code terminal but not developers
- Sessions are club time, not timetabled lessons — engagement must be intrinsic, not compliance-based
- Materials must be interactive and visually engaging — these students chose this club, so it needs to feel worth their time
- The programme should work whether students attend every week or miss occasionally
- Sessions are approximately 45-50 minutes of working time
