Budget & Cash Flow

Build a 12-month cash flow forecast for a growing UK startup. The same task a junior FP&A analyst does every month — except they take a day and you'll do it in 30 minutes.

Your Role
Junior Financial Planning & Analysis Analyst
Helix Labs — a 30-person UK SaaS startup based in Manchester

You joined Helix Labs two weeks ago. The company sells project management software to small construction firms. Revenue is growing but cash is tight — they're burning through reserves faster than new subscriptions come in. The CFO, Sarah Chen, needs clarity on whether the company can survive the next 12 months without raising more money.

The Brief — From Sarah Chen, CFO

I need a 12-month cash flow forecast on my desk by end of day. The board meets Thursday and they'll want to see three scenarios: base case (current trajectory), optimistic (we land two enterprise deals in Q2), and pessimistic (we lose our biggest client in month 4).

I've attached last quarter's actuals and our current expense breakdown. The numbers are real — don't smooth them out. If we're going to run out of cash, I need to know which month and how much runway we have left.

One more thing: the board will ask about break-even. When do we stop burning cash? Give me a clear answer with the assumptions behind it.

Why This Matters — The Real World

This is one of the first tasks a junior finance hire does at any company. Before AI, it took a graduate analyst a full day to build a cash flow model in Excel. JPMorgan's internal AI tool now does the first draft in under 2 minutes. PwC cut graduate hiring by 32% partly because AI handles this kind of work. The analysts who survived the cut aren't the ones who can build spreadsheets — they're the ones who can interrogate the model, challenge the assumptions, and explain what the numbers mean.
